Life Expectancy Evolution: Between Past and Future

Overview & Definition

The life expectancy, in simple terms, is the average number of years a newborn is expected to live, assuming that the death rates the infant faces throughout its life remain the same as those of the year of birth. It's a key indicator reflecting the health, healthcare, and living conditions of a country or region.


So, how is life expectancy calculated?


The calculation of life expectancy is based on life tables, which are statistical charts detailing the probability of death for a specific population at each age group. For instance, a life table for a country in a particular year might show a mortality rate of 0.005 for infants aged 0-1 years and 0.001 for children aged 1-2 years, and so on. These death probabilities are based on actual statistical data.


Put simply, life expectancy is the number of years a person is expected to live based on current age, considering the various age-specific risks of death they may face throughout their life.

Historical Review: Life Expectancy Increased in All Countries of the World

From 1820 to 2020, the global life expectancy experienced significant growth. In 1820, the life expectancy in most countries was only 30 years, with no country exceeding 60 years.


However, by 1920, thanks to the advancements of modernization and industrialization, developed countries saw life expectancies surpassing 70 years. Yet, countries like India and China progressed more slowly, leading to noticeable global disparities in living standards.


From 1920 to 2020, many countries that were once considered developing achieved rapid health advancements, dramatically reducing health disparities.


While some nations, primarily in Africa, still lag behind, many countries that were formerly classified as developing have now caught up.


Today, the difference in global life expectancy is smaller than ever before, and the average life expectancy has reached unprecedented levels.


Now, humanity has achieved unparalleled health equality while also reaching higher average life expectancies.

Disparity & Inequality: The Existence of Unequal Life Expectancy Within the Same Country

In this context, we delved into the phenomenon of life expectancy disparities and inequalities within the same country. Similar to how we measure income distribution inequality, we employed the Atkinson index to quantify the extent of life expectancy inequality.


The central idea behind this approach is to estimate the substantial "health reservoir" concentrated within a minority of the population in a given country, resulting in significantly longer lifespans compared to the majority within the same country.


The plot above presents estimations of this level of life expectancy inequality as measured by the Atkinson index. Here, a high Atkinson index indicates a highly unequal distribution of life years, signifying substantial disparities in the number of years people live within the same country.

These estimations are sourced from Peltzman (2009), where you can find more comprehensive details regarding the underlying data and estimation methodologies.


As the chart illustrates, there has been a significant reduction in health outcome inequalities within many countries. This observation draws our attention to the fact that, despite the overall increase in average life expectancy globally, disparities in life expectancy persist within the same countries.


This exploration of the issue is essential for our understanding of societal health disparities within a single nation and provides valuable insights for policymaking and interventions. We can underscores the significance of global efforts to enhance overall health levels, ensuring equal opportunities for health and longevity among people in different regions and social groups.
