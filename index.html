Life Expectancy Evolution: Between Past and Future

Overview & Definition

The life expectancy, in simple terms, is the average number of years a newborn is expected to live, assuming that the death rates the infant faces throughout its life remain the same as those of the year of birth. It's a key indicator reflecting the health, healthcare, and living conditions of a country or region.


So, how is life expectancy calculated?


The calculation of life expectancy is based on life tables, which are statistical charts detailing the probability of death for a specific population at each age group. For instance, a life table for a country in a particular year might show a mortality rate of 0.005 for infants aged 0-1 years and 0.001 for children aged 1-2 years, and so on. These death probabilities are based on actual statistical data.


With these mortality rates, we can determine the average expected years of life for each age group. By summing up the average expected years of life for all age groups, we get the total life expectancy from birth.


Put simply, life expectancy is the number of years a person is expected to live based on current age, considering the various age-specific risks of death they may face throughout their life. Historical Review: Life Expectancy Increased in All Countries of the World

From 1820 to the present, the global changes in life expectancy reflect significant advancements in human health and development. In 1820, the life expectancy in most countries, such as India and South Korea, was only 28 years, and no country exceeded 60 years. By 1920, with the progress of modernization and industrialization, developed countries saw life expectancies surpassing 70 years.


However, countries like India and China progressed slowly, leading to significant global disparities in living standards. Yet, from 1920 to 2020, formerly developing countries made the swiftest advancements, dramatically reducing health disparities. While some nations (primarily in Africa) remain behind, many of the once-developing countries have caught up.


Now, the world sees smaller life expectancy gaps than ever before, but the average life expectancy has reached unprecedented heights. Over time, from 1820 to 2020, life expectancy in countries worldwide has significantly increased.


Although there were pronounced health disparities in 1920, by 2020, these disparities had considerably diminished, with most countries witnessing substantial health advancements. Today, humanity has achieved unparalleled equality in health, all while attaining a higher average life expectancy.
